Background and Motivation

The world is all about what’s trending. As millenials, staying up to date on what is trending is essential to social things; knowledge of these things is like currency. YouTube is the largest video sharing website, with 5 hours of video uploaded every second. Traditional TV is phasing out due to younger generations of content consumers, and YouTube's lagless direct-to-consumer model. If something noteworthy happens, YouTube users immediately upload their angle on the event.

Project Objectives

We would like to visualize what varieties of video content are trending at a given time, and to see how this breakdown differs regionally. The biggest benefit is to know what is trending, and where. This is important for advertisers as ads are being tailored the consumers they are shown to; knowing what content is trending for certain groups of users allows advertisers to refine in another way. This visualization may also help content creators tailor their content to increase their viewership. For average consumers of YouTube content, this could also provide insight into how the content landscape changes over time. The types of videos that generated the most clicks a year ago are certainly different than the most popular videos today, but how do these types of changes occur? Does the breakdown change significantly every minute? How about every hour? Or day? A sudden change might mean that something big just happened.

Data

We are utilizing YouTube’s API to collect data about trending videos. Their REST API contains an endpoint that allows us to list the most popular videos, located at https://developers.google.com/youtube/v3/docs/videos/list. To get a human-readable string for the video category, we'll also be making a call to the endpoint documented here https://developers.google.com/youtube/v3/docs/videoCategories/list.

Data Processing

The main source of data will be from the YouTube API.  All computation will be done client-side, and the first step in data processing will be writing our own wrapper around the basic functionalities provided by Google's JavaScript client library. We will use this wrapper to get the current most popular videos, and we expect to make this call about once every second to feed the visualization. Once we have the raw data from the API, we will then aggregate the videos by category, based on the category id that each YouTube video has. After counting the total number of views that the top (50, subject to change) videos have, we update the stacked area chart using the proportion of the views that each category received.

Visualization Design

We will use a world map that the user can use to focus on a specific country, and we will use a stacked area chart to display the video view data. The world map gives users an intuitive way to select a country while the stacked area chart provides several benefits. Our focus graph needs to encode three key components: the total number of views, the proportion of each category, and the change over time. We decided to use a stacked area chart, where the horizontal axis will be time and the vertical axis will be total number of views. Thus, the height of the overall area encodes the total number of views while the individual segments will represent the proportion of each of the categories.

This design is superior to something like a pie chart, which would only effectively encode the proportion of each category (the overall size of the pie could encode total views, but the height of the stacked area chart is both more intuitive and more scalable). This would require something like a slider to compare change over time.

Another similar design is to use a stacked bar chart instead of a pie. To encode the total number of views, we would add an axis below the bar which would change when the time slider is moved and the total number of views changes.

Our third alternative is similar to our final design. Instead of a stacked area chart, we use a stacked bar chart at each of the time points that we collect data. While this might be more 'honest' with the data that we have (since we only have the data at discrete time points, the area chart has to do some kind of interpolation between those points), it would likely be much harder to parse the information for the user.

These designs are contained in the folder with the writeup.

Milestone 1 - Visual Update

This area chart works, and looks good with false youtube trending data. It charts the number of views of the top 50 trending videos, and organizes them by category; the fake data shows four of these categories. However, when we perform API calls to YouTube, the data is updated by YouTube so slowly that the area chart flatlines. We have ideas for solutions to make our data more interesting: Put the visualization on an Amazon ec2, and make an API call every 12 hours, and save the data so we can animate its appearance. This way the data has a more visually stimulating appearance.
